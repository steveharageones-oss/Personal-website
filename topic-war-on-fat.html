The War on Fat: A Manufactured Crisis

The demonization of saturated fat and the promotion of seed oils as "heart healthy" represents one of the most consequential public health errors in modern history. This transformation of dietary wisdom was not an organic scientific consensus but a manufactured narrative driven by industry interests, ideological commitment, and the systematic suppression of contradictory evidence.

Overview

Executive Summary

The key findings of this investigation reveal:

  • Ancel Keys cherry-picked data from 7 of 22 available countries to support his lipid hypothesis
  • Procter & Gamble donated $1.7 million ($20 million today) to the American Heart Association in 1948, beginning decades of industry influence
  • Project 226 (1965): The sugar industry paid Harvard scientists to shift blame from sugar to saturated fat
  • Three major RCTs showing harm from PUFA substitution were buried for 40+ years
  • Researchers who dissented (Yudkin, Enig, Golomb) faced professional retaliation
  • Dietary guidelines were codified in 1977 despite weak evidence and strong contrary data

The Smoking Gun

Project 226: Sugar Industry Influence

In 1965, the Sugar Research Foundation (SRF) - the industry trade group now known as the Sugar Association - initiated "Project 226" to influence the scientific debate on heart disease causation.

The Payment

The SRF paid Harvard researchers $6,500 (equivalent to ~$50,000 today) to conduct a literature review of studies on fat, sugar, and heart disease. The researchers were:

  • D. Mark Hegsted: Future administrator of USDA nutrition division
  • Robert McGandy: Harvard School of Public Health
  • Frederick Stare: Chair of Harvard Nutrition Department

The Instruction

Internal industry documents (discovered by researcher Cristin Kearns) reveal the SRF explicitly instructed the Harvard team to:

  1. Select studies that supported the saturated fat-heart disease link
  2. Criticize studies that linked sugar to heart disease
  3. Conclude that reducing saturated fat was the key to preventing heart disease

The Result

The resulting review, published in the prestigious New England Journal of Medicine in 1967, did exactly as instructed:

  • Emphasized studies linking saturated fat to heart disease
  • Dismissed evidence for sugar's role with methodological critiques
  • Concluded that modifying dietary fat was the primary prevention strategy
  • No disclosure of industry funding

The Consequence

This single review article shaped decades of nutrition policy:

  • Cited extensively in government reports
  • Influenced the 1977 Dietary Goals
  • Diverted attention from sugar research
  • Protected sugar industry interests for 50 years

The Sugar Research Foundation noted in internal documents that the review would "dispel the mistaken notion that sugar consumption contributes to heart disease."

Foundational Fraud

Ancel Keys and the Seven Countries Fraud

The lipid hypothesis proposes a simple chain: dietary saturated fat raises blood cholesterol, which causes atherosclerosis, which leads to heart attacks. To prove this, Ancel Keys conducted the Seven Countries Study (1958-1970), which became the foundational evidence for decades of dietary advice.

The Six-Country Graph (1953)

Keys' famous 1953 paper showed a striking correlation between fat intake and heart disease mortality across six countries. The problem: Data from 22 countries were available. Keys selected 6.

The Yerushalmy & Hilleboe Critique (1957)

Two statisticians published a devastating critique:

  • When all 22 countries are included, the correlation disappears
  • Countries like France (high fat, low heart disease) and Mexico (low fat, high heart disease) contradicted Keys' thesis
  • The apparent correlation was an artifact of country selection

The Critique

"It is concluded that the association between the percent of calories from fat and heart disease is not valid." — New York State Journal of Medicine, 1957

Keys' response: Ignore the critique and proceed with the Seven Countries Study.

The Selection Problem

Keys claimed to have chosen countries randomly, but evidence suggests otherwise:

  • He excluded countries known to contradict his hypothesis
  • Within countries, he selected populations that fit his theory
  • The famous Mediterranean diet narrative emerged from selective interpretation of Greek island data

The Mediterranean Diet Problem

The now-famous "Mediterranean diet" narrative emerged from studying Greek populations on Crete during Lent, when meat and dairy intake were artificially low for religious reasons. This wasn't their normal diet — it was a religious fasting period.

Buried Evidence

The Minnesota Coronary Experiment (1968-1973)

The Minnesota Coronary Experiment (MCE) was one of the largest and most rigorous dietary trials ever conducted:

  • Participants: 9,423 institutionalized subjects
  • Setting: Six Minnesota state mental hospitals and one nursing home
  • Design: Randomized, single-blind, controlled trial
  • Funding: National Institutes of Health

The Intervention

Diet Group Saturated Fat PUFA Sources
Control~18.5% calories~4.5% caloriesStandard hospital diet
Intervention~9.2% calories~13.2% caloriesModified with corn oil, margarine

What Should Have Happened

According to the lipid hypothesis:

  1. Lower dietary saturated fat → lower serum cholesterol
  2. Lower serum cholesterol → fewer heart attacks
  3. PUFA substitution should save lives

What Actually Happened

The intervention did lower cholesterol by 13.8%. But mortality results contradicted expectations:

Outcome Control Intervention Result
Total mortalityBaselineNo significant difference-
Non-CHD mortalityBaseline71% higherp = 0.05

The Age Interaction

A critical finding emerged when results were analyzed by age:

  • Subjects < 65 years: No significant mortality difference
  • Subjects >= 65 years: Significantly higher mortality in intervention group

The Suppression

The study was never published in full:

  • 1975: Frantz presented preliminary results at AHA meeting
  • 1981: Brief report mentioned cholesterol lowering but not mortality
  • 1994: Frantz retired without publishing full results
  • 2000: Frantz died
  • 2006: Christopher Ramsden (NIH) discovered unpublished data in Frantz's garage
  • 2016: Full results finally published in BMJ — 43 years after study completion
Another Buried Trial

The Sydney Diet Heart Study (1966-1973)

  • Participants: 458 men aged 30-59 with recent coronary event
  • Setting: Sydney, Australia
  • Design: Randomized controlled trial

The Intervention

The intervention group received safflower oil (high-PUFA) and safflower oil-based margarine, with counseling to reduce saturated fat.

The Results

Outcome Control Intervention Relative Risk
All-cause mortality11.2%17.2%1.54 (p = 0.05)
CHD mortality9.0%16.3%1.74 (p = 0.04)
Cardiovascular mortality10.8%17.6%1.63 (p = 0.04)

The Key Finding

The intervention group had 74% higher CHD mortality.

The Suppression

  • 1978: Study published in Australian and New Zealand Journal of Medicine
  • Omitted: The mortality data showing harm
  • Reported: Only that intervention "appeared to reduce" cardiovascular events in subgroup
  • 2013: Christopher Ramsden recovered the original data and published true results in BMJ
The Third Buried Trial

The Anti-Coronary Club (1957-1966)

  • Participants: 944 men, ages 40-59, initially free of heart disease
  • Setting: New York City
  • Design: Prospective controlled trial (not randomized)

The "Prudent Diet"

The intervention group consumed what became the model for subsequent dietary recommendations: low saturated fat, high PUFA, low cholesterol.

The Results

Outcome Control "Prudent Diet"
Myocardial infarctionLowerHigher
Total mortality8.6%10.8%
Sudden deathLowerHigher

Total mortality was 26% higher in the "Prudent Diet" group — the diet that would become the foundation of US dietary guidelines.

The study was published in 1966 but ignored despite showing harm from the intervention.

Why RCTs Matter

Randomized controlled trials represent the gold standard in medical evidence. Unlike observational studies, RCTs:

  • Control for confounding variables
  • Demonstrate causation, not just correlation
  • Provide the strongest evidence for clinical recommendations

When three RCTs all point in the same direction — harm from PUFA substitution — and that evidence is suppressed, the consequences for public health are profound.

Corporate Intervention

Procter & Gamble and the Seed Oil Industry

In the late 19th century, Procter & Gamble was primarily a soap and candle company. The invention of the light bulb threatened their candle business, creating a need for new products.

From Candles to Crisco

In 1907, P&G acquired the rights to a German hydrogenation process that could turn liquid cottonseed oil into a solid fat. The result:

Crisco (1911): The first solid vegetable shortening made from hydrogenated cottonseed oil.

Marketing a New Product

Crisco faced significant marketing challenges:

  • Consumers were skeptical of "artificial" fats
  • Lard and butter were traditional, trusted products
  • Cottonseed oil was considered industrial waste

P&G's solution: Position Crisco as a healthful, modern alternative.

The American Heart Association Partnership

In 1948, P&G donated $1.7 million (equivalent to ~$20 million today) to the American Heart Association. This began decades of industry influence over what was supposed to be an independent health organization.

The AHA would go on to become one of the most influential proponents of seed oil consumption and saturated fat restriction.

Key Takeaways

The demonization of saturated fat represents one of the most significant public health errors in modern history. The evidence now clearly shows:

  1. The scientific foundation was fatally flawed — Keys' cherry-picked data contradicted by full analysis
  2. Industry influence corrupted the process — P&G, sugar industry, and pharmaceutical interests shaped outcomes
  3. Contrary evidence was systematically suppressed — Three RCTs showing harm buried for 40+ years
  4. Modern research validates the critics — 2020-2025 meta-analyses confirm no mortality benefit from saturated fat restriction
  5. The guidelines failed their purpose — Obesity and diabetes exploded under low-fat dietary advice

Perhaps most importantly, this history serves as a cautionary tale about the vulnerability of science to industry influence, confirmation bias, and institutional inertia.
