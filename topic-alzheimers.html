Alzheimer's: The "Type 3 Diabetes" Epidemic

For decades, Alzheimer's Disease has been treated primarily as a localized genetic or protein misfolding issue (the amyloid plaque hypothesis). However, emerging clinical framework recharacterizes it as a systemic metabolic disease driven by brain insulin resistance and glucose hypometabolism.

At a glance

Why this topic matters

Alzheimer's Disease is now the 6th leading cause of death in the United States, and rates are skyrocketing in parallel with modern metabolic diseases like obesity and Type 2 Diabetes.

Rather than a mysterious disease of old age, neurodegeneration is increasingly recognized as the end-stage result of chronic energy failure in the brain. When neurons become insulin resistant, they lose the ability to utilize glucose effectively, essentially starving to death while swimming in an ocean of circulating blood sugar.

The Core Mechanism

Brain Insulin Resistance

Insulin is the hormone responsible for unlocking cells to let glucose in for energy. Decades of consuming refined carbohydrates, liquid sugar, and chronic snacking keep insulin levels artificially elevated all day long.

Just as the liver and muscle cells eventually become "deaf" to insulin to protect themselves from excess energy (Type 2 Diabetes), the neurons in the brain do the exact same thing. This phenomenon—Brain Insulin Resistance—has led researchers to dub Alzheimer's as Type 3 Diabetes. PET scans of Alzheimer's patients routinely show "dark" areas in the brain where glucose metabolism has completely flatlined years before memory loss begins.
