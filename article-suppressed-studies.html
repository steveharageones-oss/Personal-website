The Suppressed Studies: Buried Evidence of Harm

Between 1968 and 1973, three major randomized controlled trials tested the hypothesis that replacing saturated fat with polyunsaturated vegetable oil would reduce heart disease. All three produced results contradicting the lipid hypothesis. All three were buried.

At a glance

Why Random Control Trials Matter

Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) represent the gold standard in medical evidence. Unlike observational studies, RCTs control for confounding variables and demonstrate causation, not just correlation.

When three of the most highly funded, rigorously designed RCTs in history all point in the same direction—harm from Polyunsaturated Fatty Acid (PUFA) substitution—and that evidence is suppressed, the consequences for public health are profound.

The 43-Year Cover-Up

The Minnesota Coronary Experiment (1968–1973)

One of the largest dietary trials ever conducted, funded by the NIH and led by Ivan Frantz Jr., involved 9,423 institutionalized subjects. The intervention diet replaced saturated fat (butter, beef fat) with polyunsaturated fat (corn oil, margarine).

The Results: The intervention group successfully lowered their serum cholesterol by 13.8%. However, instead of saving lives, the intervention group experienced a 71% higher non-cardiac mortality rate. Furthermore, subjects over the age of 65 saw significantly amplified mortality risk.

The Suppression: The results were not published. Decades later, Frantz died without formally releasing the full findings. In 2016, NIH researcher Christopher Ramsden discovered the raw data tapes in Frantz's garage, analyzed them, and published the true outcomes in the BMJ—43 years late.

Omitted Mortality Data

The Sydney Diet Heart Study (1966–1973)

Involving 458 men with a recent coronary event, this trial replaced saturated fat with safflower oil and safflower-based margarine.

The Results: The intervention group had a 74% higher Coronary Heart Disease (CHD) mortality rate, and a 54% higher all-cause mortality rate compared to the control group.

The Suppression: When published in 1978, the researchers actively omitted the mortality data showing harm, instead stating the intervention "appeared to reduce" cardiovascular events in a tiny subgroup. The buried mortality data was recovered and published in 2013 by ramsden.

The "Prudent Diet" Failure

The Anti-Coronary Club (1957–1966)

Led by George Christakis in New York City with 944 men, this prospective controlled trial placed subjects on what was termed the "Prudent Diet" (low saturated fat, high PUFA from industrial oils).

The Results: While myocardial infarctions appeared slightly lower, the overall total mortality rate was higher in the Prudent Diet group (10.8%) compared to the control group eating the standard American diet of the era (8.6%). Despite showing net harm, the Prudent Diet became the model for subsequent dietary recommendations.
