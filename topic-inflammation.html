Inflammation: We're Treating the Wrong Problem

You've probably heard of prostaglandins-the inflammation molecules that ibuprofen blocks. But there's another class of inflammatory compounds in your blood right now, present at thousands of times higher levels, that no drug can touch. They come from your food. And almost nobody is studying them.

Starting Point

The Big Picture

When you take an ibuprofen for a headache or joint pain, you're blocking molecules called prostaglandins. These are messengers your body produces to signal inflammation. The drug works because it stops an enzyme (COX) from making these messengers.

But here's what nobody talks about: prostaglandins are just one source of inflammation. There's another source-and it's vastly more abundant in your body.

These other compounds are called OXLAMs (Oxidized Linoleic Acid Metabolites, but don't worry about the name yet). The crucial difference is this:

  • Prostaglandins are produced on purpose by your body, in controlled amounts, through enzymes
  • OXLAMs are produced by accident, in uncontrolled amounts, through damage to dietary fats

And OXLAMs are present in your blood at levels roughly 12,000 times higher than prostaglandins.

Part 1

A Quick Primer on Fats

To understand what's happening, we need to cover a few basics about the fats in your diet.

Saturated vs. Unsaturated Fats

Think of fat molecules like chains. A saturated fat has all its links filled - every carbon atom has its full quota of hydrogen atoms. This makes the chain stable, like a solid brick.

An unsaturated fat has gaps - places where hydrogen atoms are missing. These gaps create weak points in the chain, making the fat much more vulnerable to damage.

The more gaps (double bonds), the more unstable the fat:

Fat Double Bonds Stability
Butter, coconut oil0 (saturated)Very stable
Olive oil1 (monounsaturated)Moderately stable
Seed oils (soybean, canola, etc.)2-6 (polyunsaturated)Highly unstable

Linoleic Acid: The Fat We're Drowning In

Linoleic acid is an omega-6 polyunsaturated fat with two double bonds. It's the predominant fat in industrial seed oils like soybean, corn, canola, sunflower, and cottonseed oil.

These oils are everywhere in the modern diet-in restaurant food, processed snacks, salad dressings, and most packaged foods. The average American now consumes about 66 pounds of these oils per year.

Because linoleic acid has those weak points (double bonds), it's highly vulnerable to a process called oxidation.

Part 2

What Is Oxidation?

Oxidation is essentially the same process that makes metal rust or makes avocados turn brown. When unstable molecules are exposed to oxygen, light, or heat, they break apart and change into something else.

When linoleic acid oxidizes, it doesn't just disappear. It breaks into smaller pieces called OXLAMs (Oxidized Linoleic Acid Metabolites).

This can happen:

  • During manufacturing: Seed oils are heated to high temperatures during extraction and refining
  • During cooking: Heating seed oils in a pan speeds up oxidation dramatically
  • Inside your body: At body temperature (98 F), linoleic acid in your tissues continues oxidizing slowly but constantly

Think of it like this: linoleic acid is a fragile glass, and heat, light, and oxygen are hammers. Once the glass shatters, you can't put it back together.

Part 3

What Are OXLAMs?

OXLAMs are the broken pieces left over when linoleic acid is damaged by oxidation. Unlike prostaglandins, which your body manufactures deliberately to send signals, OXLAMs are essentially accident products-the wreckage left behind.

Your body has no enzyme that produces OXLAMs. They form randomly, uncontrollably, wherever unstable fats encounter oxygen and heat.

The major OXLAMs include compounds you might see in research: 4-HNE, 9-HODE, 13-HODE, and MDA. But what matters more than their names is what they do:

How OXLAMs Cause Problems

  • They damage cells: OXLAMs can react with proteins and DNA, changing how cells function
  • They trigger inflammation: Unlike prostaglandins (which signal inflammation), OXLAMs directly cause inflammatory responses
  • They accumulate: Your body has ways to clear them, but when production exceeds clearance, they build up
  • They're linked to disease: Higher OXLAM levels are found in people with heart disease, liver disease, Alzheimer's, and cancer

A Crucial Distinction

Prostaglandins = controlled inflammation signals, like a text message your body sends on purpose

OXLAMs = uncontrolled inflammation triggers, like smoke from a fire that started by accident

Part 4

What Are Prostaglandins?

Prostaglandins are messenger molecules that your body deliberately produces to coordinate various functions. They're made from a fat called arachidonic acid through a controlled process involving enzymes called COX-1 and COX-2.

These molecules serve normal, healthy purposes:

  • Protecting the lining of your stomach
  • Helping blood clot when you're injured
  • Regulating kidney function
  • Signaling pain and inflammation (when appropriate)

When you're injured or fighting an infection, prostaglandins help orchestrate the healing response. That's useful. But when prostaglandin levels stay too high for too long, you get chronic inflammation, pain, and swelling.

The Pharmaceutical Solution: NSAIDs

Drugs like ibuprofen, aspirin, and naproxen work by blocking the COX enzymes. Without COX, your body can't make prostaglandins. Less prostaglandins means less inflammation and pain.

But there's a catch-or several:

NSAIDs Have Consequences

  • Since prostaglandins protect your stomach, blocking them can cause ulcers and GI bleeding
  • About 16,500 Americans die each year from NSAID-related GI complications
  • NSAIDs don't address the cause of inflammation-only one of the messengers

Most importantly: NSAIDs do absolutely nothing about OXLAMs.

Part 5

The Concentration Gap

Here's where things get striking. Researchers can measure both prostaglandins and OXLAMs in blood samples. The difference in quantity is enormous.

Putting It in Perspective

If you took a drop of blood and counted both types of molecules:

  • Prostaglandins: roughly 3 to 12 molecules per unit
  • OXLAMs: roughly 150,000 molecules per unit

That's roughly 12,000 times more OXLAMs than prostaglandins.

Think of it this way:

  • Imagine prostaglandins as the volume of a quiet conversation
  • OXLAMs, by comparison, would be the volume of a roaring jet engine

We've built a $22+ billion industry around blocking the quiet conversation, while almost completely ignoring the jet engine.

Part 6

Why There's No Drug for OXLAMs

The reason prostaglandins have a drug and OXLAMs don't comes down to how they're made.

Prostaglandins: A Drug Target Exists

Prostaglandins are produced by enzymes (COX-1 and COX-2). Enzymes are like machines - specific tools that do specific jobs. If you want to stop prostaglandin production, you just need to gum up the machine. That's what ibuprofen does.

This creates a pharmaceutical opportunity: invent a drug that blocks the enzyme, sell it to millions of people, generate billions in profit. Done.

OXLAMs: No Machine to Block

OXLAMs aren't made by enzymes. They're made by random chemical reactions-the same process that makes iron rust or butter go rancid.

There's no specific machine to shut off. The only way to reduce OXLAM production is to reduce the raw material: linoleic acid in your body.

And that means eating less seed oil.

The Inconvenient Truth

If the solution is dietary, there's no patentable drug to sell.

No drug company will spend billions researching something that ends with "eat different food." There's no return on investment.

Part 7

The Industry Connection

The seed oil industry is vast. The global market for vegetable oils is estimated at $280-369 billion annually - roughly 12 to 15 times larger than the entire market for anti-inflammatory drugs.

This creates a peculiar dynamic:

  1. The food industry profits from selling seed oils
  2. Seed oils increase OXLAM levels in the body
  3. OXLAMs contribute to inflammation and chronic disease
  4. The pharmaceutical industry profits from treating inflammation symptoms

Nobody profits from asking whether the seed oils are the problem in the first place.

Industry-Funded Research

When an industry funds research, the results tend to favor that industry. Studies show that industry-funded nutrition research is 4 to 8 times more likely to reach conclusions favorable to the sponsor.

The seed oil industry-through organizations like the Soy Nutrition Institute, funded by the United Soybean Board-sponsors research and reviews that minimize concerns about linoleic acid and OXLAMs.

Example: A Common "Nothing to Worry About" Study Design

  • Measure standard markers like CRP (C-reactive protein) and cholesterol
  • Find that seed oils don't dramatically increase these markers
  • Conclude seed oils are safe

What's missing: OXLAMs were never measured.

Part 8

What Actually Works

If OXLAMs come from linoleic acid, then reducing linoleic acid should reduce OXLAMs. This has actually been tested in clinical trials.

The OXLAM Reduction Study

In a controlled study, participants reduced their linoleic acid intake from about 6.7% of calories to 2.4% of calories. The result:

OXLAM levels dropped by 13.1%

This proves OXLAM levels are modifiable through diet. They're not fixed-you can reduce them by changing what you eat.

Another Study That Raised Questions

The Sydney Diet Heart Study, conducted in the 1960s-70s, tested whether replacing saturated fat with linoleic acid (from seed oils) would reduce heart disease. The data was "recovered" decades later and re-analyzed.

The finding: The group eating more linoleic acid actually had higher rates of death from heart disease and all causes.

This wasn't what researchers expected, and the full data wasn't published at the time. It was only re-analyzed and published in 2013.

Summary

The Takeaway

Key Points

  1. Two sources of inflammation: Prostaglandins (controlled, enzyme-driven) and OXLAMs (uncontrolled, from oxidized dietary fats)
  2. Massive difference in amount: OXLAMs are roughly 12,000 times more abundant in blood than prostaglandins
  3. Drug exists for one: NSAIDs block prostaglandins, creating a $22+ billion market
  4. No drug for the other: OXLAMs form through random oxidation-no enzyme to block
  5. The solution is dietary: Reducing linoleic acid (seed oils) lowers OXLAM levels
  6. Industry dynamics: Food industry profits from seed oils; pharma industry profits from treating inflammation symptoms; neither profits from solving the root cause

The Core Question

Modern medicine has chosen to ask: "How can we drug away inflammation?"

A more complete question might be: "Why is there so much inflammation to drug away?"

The answer might be sitting in your kitchen cabinet.

Practical Steps

What Can You Do?

If you want to reduce OXLAM exposure, the most effective approach is to reduce your intake of linoleic acid-the raw material OXLAMs are made from.

High-Linoleic Acid Oils to Minimize

  • Soybean oil
  • Corn oil
  • Canola oil
  • Sunflower oil
  • Cottonseed oil
  • Safflower oil
  • Grapeseed oil
  • "Vegetable oil" (typically soybean)

Lower-Linoleic Alternatives

  • Olive oil: Mostly monounsaturated, low in linoleic acid
  • Avocado oil: Similar to olive oil
  • Coconut oil: Saturated, very stable, almost no linoleic acid
  • Butter or ghee: Low in linoleic acid (especially from grass-fed sources)
  • Tallow or lard: Traditional fats low in linoleic acid
