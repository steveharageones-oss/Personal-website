Topic dossier

Seed Oils: The OXLAM Crisis

How an industrial waste product became a food staple—and triggered the greatest metabolic catastrophe in human history.

At a glance

Why this topic matters

The conventional narrative suggests that as heart disease rates soared in the mid-20th century, scientists organically discovered that saturated fat was the culprit. Re-evaluating historical records presents a different picture entirely.

Corporate financial interventions—such as Procter & Gamble's $1.7 million donation to the American Heart Association in 1948 and the sugar industry's covert funding of Harvard researchers in 1965—played pivotal roles in cementing the lipid hypothesis into public consciousness.

The Methodology

Ancel Keys and the Seven Countries Fraud

The lipid hypothesis proposes a simple chain: dietary saturated fat raises blood cholesterol, which causes atherosclerosis, which leads to heart attacks. To prove this, Ancel Keys conducted the Seven Countries Study (1958-1970), which became the foundational evidence for decades of dietary advice.

However, this study is now widely considered one of the most consequential examples of scientific cherry-picking:

  • Keys had access to data from 22 countries but systematically selected only the 7 that perfectly supported his hypothesis.
  • When retrospective analyses plotted all 22 data points, the correlation between fat intake and heart disease effectively disappeared.
  • The now-famous "Mediterranean diet" narrative emerged from studying Greek populations on Crete during Lent, when meat and dairy intake were artificially low for religious reasons.
Corporate intervention

Project 226 and the Sugar Industry

While the focus intensified on saturated fat, compelling evidence linking sugar to rising heart disease rates was actively suppressed. In 1965, the Sugar Research Foundation (now the Sugar Association) initiated "Project 226."

They paid Harvard scientists (the equivalent of $50,000 today) to publish a heavily biased two-part literature review in the New England Journal of Medicine. This review systematically minimized the link between sugar and cardiovascular disease, shifting the blame almost entirely onto saturated fat.

The Aftermath

Suppression and Lasting Consequences

Dissenting researchers who highlighted these methodological flaws—such as John Yudkin and Mary Enig—faced severe professional retaliation, loss of funding, and marginalization from the scientific community.

Even more troubling, three major Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) showing active harm from substituting saturated fat with polyunsaturated fats (PUFAs) were buried and left unpublished for over 40 years. Despite this remarkably weak evidentiary base, these skewed findings were codified into the first U.S. Dietary Goals in 1977, transforming agricultural surplus products into mandated "health foods."
